A quick refresher on the frameworks you'll apply in this simulation.

Now / Next / Later Roadmaps

Time-horizon planning without false precision

Traditional roadmaps with specific dates create two problems: they commit teams to solutions before they understand the problem, and they set expectations that rarely survive contact with reality. The Now/Next/Later framework groups work by time horizon rather than date, giving teams clarity on priorities without the rigidity of a Gantt chart. "Now" is committed work in progress. "Next" is validated problems queued for the next cycle. "Later" is strategic bets under exploration.

How to apply it

Define clear criteria for what moves between horizons. "Now" items should have defined scope, assigned teams, and success metrics. "Next" items need validated user problems and rough sizing. "Later" items are strategic hypotheses that need research before committing resources. Review the roadmap quarterly: promote, demote, or remove items based on new data.

Example

Shopify's product team uses outcome-based planning rather than feature-based timelines. When building Shopify Payments, the roadmap wasn't "launch payment processing by Q3." It was: "Now — reduce checkout abandonment by 15% with integrated payments. Next — expand to 5 new countries. Later — explore embedded financial services for merchants." Each horizon had different levels of certainty and commitment.

Now / Next / Later Template

  1. Now (0-6 weeks) — Committed. Scoped. Team assigned. Success metrics defined. Shipping this cycle.
  2. Next (6-12 weeks) — Validated problem. Solution exploration in progress. Roughly sized. Queued for next cycle.
  3. Later (3-6 months) — Strategic hypothesis. Needs research/discovery. No commitment yet. May be promoted or killed.
  4. Horizon Criteria — What evidence moves an item from Later → Next? From Next → Now? Define promotion triggers.
  5. Review Cadence — Weekly standup on Now. Biweekly review of Next. Monthly strategic review of Later.
  6. Stakeholder View — Engineers see Now detail. Execs see all three. Merchants see Now + directional Next.
  7. Kill Criteria — When do you remove items entirely? Data disproves hypothesis? Strategy shift? Resource constraint?

Outcome-Based Roadmapping

Roadmap what you'll achieve, not what you'll build

Feature roadmaps answer "What are we building?" Outcome roadmaps answer "What will change for our users and business?" The difference matters because features are bets — you think they'll drive the outcome, but you might be wrong. When you commit to outcomes instead of features, teams have freedom to find the best solution rather than building a predetermined feature that may not work.

How to apply it

Frame every roadmap item as a measurable outcome: "Reduce merchant onboarding time from 3 days to 3 hours" not "Build new onboarding wizard." Define key results that prove the outcome is achieved. Let teams propose and iterate on solutions. If the first solution doesn't move the metric, they pivot — the roadmap commitment is to the outcome, not the feature.

Example

When Shopify planned its international expansion, the outcome wasn't "launch in 10 new countries." It was "enable 100,000 merchants outside North America to reach $1M in GMV within 12 months." This outcome framing meant the team could pursue multiple strategies — local payment methods, translated checkout, regional shipping integrations — and measure success by merchant revenue, not country count.

Outcome-Based Roadmap Template

  1. Strategic Theme — Which company objective does this serve? (e.g., "Merchant retention" or "International growth")
  2. Target Outcome — Measurable change in user behaviour or business metric. Specific, time-bound, ambitious but achievable.
  3. Key Results — 2-3 metrics that prove the outcome is happening. Leading indicators preferred over lagging.
  4. Current State — Where are we today on this metric? What's the baseline? Why is the current state unacceptable?
  5. Solution Hypotheses — 2-3 potential approaches. These are bets, not commitments. Will be validated through discovery.
  6. Dependencies — What other teams, platforms, or decisions does this outcome depend on?
  7. Investment Level — T-shirt size (S/M/L/XL). How many people, for how long? Rough order of magnitude.
  8. Confidence Level — High/Medium/Low. How sure are we this outcome is achievable? What would increase confidence?

Roadmap Trade-offs

Every "yes" is a hundred "no"s — making trade-offs explicit

The hardest part of roadmapping isn't deciding what to build — it's deciding what NOT to build and defending that decision. Every item on your roadmap displaces something else. Stakeholders, merchants, and executives all have legitimate needs that compete for limited engineering capacity. The PM's job isn't to make everyone happy — it's to make the trade-offs explicit and ensure the highest-impact work gets done first.

How to apply it

For every roadmap decision, document what you're NOT doing and why. Create a "not doing" list that's as visible as your roadmap. Use a consistent scoring framework (RICE, ICE, or weighted scoring) so trade-offs are based on data, not politics. When stakeholders push back, show the trade-off: "We can do X, but it means delaying Y by one quarter. Here's the impact of each."

Example

Shopify constantly balances its roadmap between serving existing SMB merchants (retention) and building for enterprise merchants via Shopify Plus (expansion). When the team decided to invest heavily in Shopify Plus features like B2B wholesale channels and custom checkout extensibility, they explicitly deprioritised several SMB-requested features. The trade-off was documented: "Enterprise GMV is growing 3x faster than SMB. Each Plus merchant generates 50x the revenue of an SMB merchant. This quarter, we prioritise Plus."

Roadmap Trade-off Framework

  1. Impact Scoring — Score each item on reach, impact, confidence, and effort (RICE). Make the math visible.
  2. Strategic Alignment — Does this item serve a top-3 company objective? If not, it needs exceptional justification.
  3. Opportunity Cost — What gets delayed or killed if we do this? Name the specific items displaced.
  4. Reversibility — Is this decision reversible? Irreversible decisions deserve more analysis. Reversible ones deserve speed.
  5. Stakeholder Impact — Who benefits? Who's harmed? Are we over-indexing on one stakeholder group?
  6. Not-Doing List — Publish what you're explicitly NOT building this quarter. Explain why. Review quarterly.
  7. Escalation Path — When stakeholders disagree with trade-offs, what's the escalation process? Who decides?

Platform Roadmap Evolution

Growing a platform without breaking what works

Platform roadmaps are uniquely complex because every change affects multiple stakeholders: end users, developers building on your platform, internal teams, and partners. A new API version that excites developers might break existing integrations. A feature that delights new merchants might confuse power users. Platform roadmapping requires thinking in ecosystems, not features — every change ripples outward.

How to apply it

Categorise roadmap items by their blast radius: internal-only changes, merchant-facing changes, developer-facing changes, and ecosystem-wide changes. Each category needs different communication timelines, migration paths, and feedback loops. For breaking changes, provide 6-12 months notice, migration tools, and clear deprecation schedules. Never surprise your ecosystem.

Example

Shopify's App Store ecosystem has 8,000+ third-party apps built by developers who depend on Shopify's APIs. When Shopify redesigned its checkout architecture (Checkout Extensibility), the platform team provided an 18-month migration timeline, extensive documentation, migration tools, and partner support. They couldn't just ship the new checkout — they had to roadmap the migration itself, treating developers as first-class stakeholders in the platform evolution.

Platform Evolution Checklist

  1. Ecosystem Audit — Who builds on your platform? How many apps/integrations? What breaks if you change X?
  2. Blast Radius — Internal only? Merchant-facing? Developer-facing? Ecosystem-wide? Each needs different process.
  3. Migration Path — For breaking changes: deprecation timeline, migration tools, documentation, support plan.
  4. Communication Plan — Developers need 6-12 months notice. Merchants need 3 months. Internal teams need 1 month.
  5. Versioning Strategy — API versioning, feature flags, gradual rollout. How do old and new coexist during transition?
  6. Feedback Loops — Developer forums, merchant advisory boards, beta programmes. How do stakeholders influence the roadmap?
  7. Success Metrics — Adoption rate of new features, migration completion %, developer satisfaction, ecosystem health.
